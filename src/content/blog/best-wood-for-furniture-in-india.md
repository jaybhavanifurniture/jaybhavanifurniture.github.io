## Hardwood vs. Softwood — The Distinction That Actually Matters

The names are misleading. "Hardwood" doesn't mean the wood is physically hard to the touch — some softwoods feel firm too. The real difference is in the wood's cellular structure. Hardwoods have a denser, more complex network of vessels, which gives them better load-bearing strength and wear resistance over time. Softwoods grow faster, cost less, and are more prone to denting under daily use.

For furniture that gets used every day — dining tables, beds, seating — this density difference is why hardwood furniture holds up in high-traffic family homes in a way cheaper softwood alternatives don't. It's not marketing language. It's the wood's cell structure meeting the reality of daily use.

## The Woods You'll Actually Encounter in India

**Teak** — Naturally oily, moisture-resistant, low maintenance. The safe choice for humid climates and rooms with heavy exposure to moisture. Straighter, more uniform grain.

**Sheesham (Indian Rosewood)** — Dense, strong, with dramatic interlocked grain that gives every piece a slightly different character. Needs proper seasoning to avoid warping, but rewards you with a look that's hard to replicate.

**Mango wood** — Softer and more affordable, decent for lower-traffic decorative pieces, less suited to daily-use dining furniture or anything that takes weight and wear.

**Acacia** — Reasonably strong, often used as a mid-range option between mango and the hardwoods above.

If you want one rule to walk away with: **for anything that gets touched, sat on, or leaned on every day, spend the extra money on teak or sheesham.** For decorative or low-use pieces, the cheaper woods are a reasonable compromise.

## The Question Nobody Asks: Solid Wood or Veneer?

This matters more than the wood species. A lot of furniture marketed as "sheesham" or "teak" is actually a veneer — a thin layer of that wood glued over a cheaper core (plywood, MDF, or particle board). It looks identical in a photo. It behaves completely differently over five years.

How to check before you buy:

*   **Lift a drawer or a corner piece.** Solid wood feels noticeably heavier and denser.
*   **Look at the edges.** Veneer often shows a visible seam or a slightly different grain pattern where the sheet wraps around a corner.
*   **Ask directly.** A real workshop will tell you exactly which parts of a piece are solid wood and which are engineered — table legs and frames are sometimes solid while panels are veneer, and that's a legitimate construction choice as long as you know about it upfront.

Neither approach is inherently a scam — veneer over a quality core can be a reasonable cost-saving choice for the right piece. The problem is when it's sold to you as solid wood without disclosure.

## Seasoning — The Step That Determines If Your Furniture Warps

Wood that hasn't been properly dried before it's carved will move after it reaches your home — cracking, warping, or drawers that stop closing properly six months in. This has nothing to do with which species you picked. A poorly seasoned piece of teak will fail faster than a well-seasoned piece of mango wood.

Ask your furniture maker: how long is the wood seasoned, and where is it sourced? If you get a vague answer, take it as a signal.
