If you've spent any time shopping for solid wood furniture in India, you've run into this question at every showroom: teak or sheesham? Salespeople usually push whichever one they have more stock of. We've been building furniture in Navsari since 1998, and we'd rather tell you the actual differences so you pick the right one for your home — not the one that's easiest for someone else to sell.

## The Short Answer

Teak is the better choice if you want low maintenance and you're furnishing a space exposed to humidity, moisture, or outdoor-adjacent conditions — balconies, ground-floor rooms, kitchens, especially in the humid coastal belt across South Gujarat (Surat, Navsari, Valsad, Vapi, Bharuch). Sheesham is the better choice if you want richer grain character, a warmer look, and you're willing to trade a bit of upkeep for that visual depth, and your budget has room to flex either way since pricing between the two overlaps more than people assume.

Neither wood is "worse." They're suited to different rooms and different owners.

## Teak: What You're Actually Paying For

Teak's reputation comes from its natural oil content. That oil is what repels moisture and keeps the wood from warping, cracking, or attracting termites the way untreated softwoods do. In a state like Gujarat, where humidity swings between the monsoon and dry season, that matters more than it does in a drier climate.

Practically, this means:

*   **Low maintenance.** You're not oiling or polishing it every season to keep it stable.
*   **Better for moisture-prone rooms.** Ground floors, homes near water, kitchens, and bathroom-adjacent furniture.
*   **Ages well without much intervention.** The color deepens naturally over years rather than degrading.
*   **Higher upfront cost, generally.** You're paying for the tree's natural chemistry, not just the carving.

The tradeoff is that teak's grain is generally straighter and more uniform. If you want furniture that's a quiet, elegant background to a room, that works in your favor. If you want a piece that's a visual centerpiece with dramatic grain, teak can look a little plain next to sheesham.

## Sheesham: What You're Actually Paying For

Sheesham — also called Indian rosewood — is denser than people expect for its price point, and its grain is where it earns its reputation. Interlocked, dark-striped, reddish-brown grain that catches light differently depending on the piece. No two sheesham tabletops look quite the same, and for a lot of our customers, that uniqueness is the entire reason they choose it.

Practically:

*   **More visual character.** If your furniture is meant to be noticed — a dining table, a console, a bed headboard — sheesham photographs and looks better in person.
*   **Very strong, comparable hardness to teak** in most day-to-day use.
*   **Needs more seasoning and care.** Sheesham that isn't properly dried before it's worked will move, crack, or warp over time. This is almost entirely dependent on your furniture maker's process, not the wood itself — which is why who builds it matters as much as which wood you pick.
*   **Generally better value for the visual impact.** You often get more "presence" per rupee with sheesham than teak.

## So Which One Should You Actually Buy?

Ask yourself three questions:

1.  **Where is it going?** Humid, moisture-heavy room → lean teak. Dry living/dining space → sheesham is fine and often the better-looking choice.
2.  **Do you want the furniture to disappear into the room, or be the room's focal point?** Disappear → teak's uniform grain. Focal point → sheesham's grain character.
3.  **How much do you trust the maker's seasoning process?** This is the one people skip. A poorly seasoned sheesham piece will cause you more problems than a well-seasoned teak piece, regardless of what the wood "should" do. Ask whoever you're buying from how long they season their wood and where it's sourced from — a real workshop will have a direct answer, not a vague one.
