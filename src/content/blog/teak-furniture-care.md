Solid teak wood, traditionally known as *Sagwan* in India, is renowned for its strength, beautiful golden-brown grains, and natural weather-resistant oils. It has been the gold standard for Indian home furniture for centuries. 

While teak wood is incredibly hardy and resistant to pests and decay, a little regular care goes a long way in preserving its rich luster and grain pattern. In South Gujarat, where we experience hot, dry summers followed by intense, humid monsoons, wood furniture undergoes seasonal movement. 

Here is our practical guide to keeping your solid teak wood furniture looking new for generations.

---

### 1. Dust Daily and Clean Gently

Dust particles may seem harmless, but they can act as micro-abrasives that scratch and dull the protective varnish or polish over time.

*   **The Golden Rule:** Use a dry, soft, lint-free microfiber cloth or a feather duster for daily cleaning.
*   **Deep Cleaning:** If the surface gets sticky or dirty, slightly damp a microfiber cloth with warm water and a drop of mild dishwashing soap. Wring it out thoroughly—the cloth should be damp, not wet. Wipe along the direction of the wood grain, and immediately follow with a dry cloth to remove any residual moisture.
*   **What to Avoid:** Never use cleaning products containing ammonia, bleach, or silicone. Silicones can create a sticky build-up that ruins the original finish.

---

### 2. Protect from Gujarat's Extreme Sun

Direct, intense sunlight is one of teak wood’s biggest enemies. The UV rays can bleach the rich golden color into a dull grey and dry out the natural oils, leading to surface cracks (checking).

If you have a teak dining table or console near a large window, use sheer curtains during peak afternoon hours. Also, rotate your furniture occasionally so that it ages uniformly if exposed to partial light.

---

### 3. Handle Spills Immediately (Blot, Don't Wipe)

Whether it’s water, tea, or food curry, spills are inevitable in an active household. Teak’s natural density helps it resist absorption, but liquid left standing will eventually leave white spots or dark rings on the finish.

*   **The Action Plan:** Keep a clean cloth handy. As soon as a spill occurs, blot the liquid gently. Do not rub or wipe aggressively, as this can press the moisture deeper into the grain or spread the spill.
*   **Protect Surfaces:** Always use coasters, heat-resistant trivets, and table mats. Placing boiling hot teacups or cold water pitchers directly on a polished wood surface can cause the finish to blush (cloud/turn white).

---

### 4. Understand Your Polish and Re-polish Wisely

Depending on how your furniture was finished at the workshop, its maintenance needs will vary:

1.  **Melamine Polish:** Popular for a durable semi-gloss finish. Needs re-polishing every 7–10 years. Clean only with a dry cloth.
2.  **PU (Polyurethane) Polish:** Highly premium, water-resistant, and scratch-resistant. PU finishes last 12–15 years and require minimal maintenance other than dusting.
3.  **Natural Oil/Wax Finish:** Rarely used for indoor furniture in India due to high maintenance, but it preserves the natural raw feel of the wood. Needs oiling (linseed or teak oil) once a year.

> **Caution:** Do not apply commercial furniture oils or polishes on top of a polyurethane (PU) or melamine coat. They cannot penetrate the sealed surface and will only leave a greasy, dust-attracting layer.

---

### 5. Prevent Termite and Moisture Damage

Although teak wood contains natural oils that deter pests, the plywood backing or adjacent walls might not be as resistant. 

*   Keep your solid wood furniture 2 to 3 inches away from damp walls. This allows air to circulate and prevents moisture from getting trapped.
*   Check the leg bases. If you mop your floors daily, make sure water does not pool around the furniture legs. Our workshop adds small nylon gliders to the bottom of all legs to elevate the wood slightly off the damp floor.
